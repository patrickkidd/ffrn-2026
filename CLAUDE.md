## Main Theses for the Day

Core arguments that should inform all presentation content:

### 1. Measurability Enables Coordination and Defuses Polarization

Everyone should be concerned with the lack of measurability in clinical psychology. Clear, objective concepts and frameworks that people can *easily agree on* remove confusion, mismatched expectations, and inefficiencies. More importantly, they **defuse polarization** and **allow people to coordinate at scale**.

**The Common Currency Analogy:** Everyone knows what a dollar is. Every dollar is the same. This enables global markets. Without common currency, you only have barter—one person's valuation against another's.

**Facts as Depolarization:** The first trick in family therapy where there are misaligned priorities and perspectives is to gather all available facts. What is a fact? An important point about the problem at hand that everyone can easily agree on. Collecting facts allows people to calm down. Like common currency, facts establish a common, calm, depolarized baseline from which people can address disagreements. Without this factual baseline, you have one person's opinions versus another's, which defaults to tribal/triangle hierarchy—emotionally determined, not rationally determined.

### 2. The Implicit Model Determines Action

"The way a person understands a problem determines what they do to solve it."

- **Implicit model**: What people actually do (often unconscious)
- **Explicit model**: What people say they do

The current implicit model in behavioral health is that the problem is inside the individual. This determines treatment approach, research design, funding, everything. Changing the implicit model changes everything.

### 3. Shift the Unit of Analysis: Individual → Family (not Society)

The proposal is NOT to jump from individual to society. The family is the smallest system where emotional process is visible. One level up from the blade of grass.

### 4. Anxiety = Perceived Uncertainty + Urgency (The "A" in SARF)

**Core concept for the day:** Anxiety occurs in response to **perceived uncertainty and urgency**. When people are anxious, the first thing they do is look for a way to reduce uncertainty.

**Social contagions (professional fads, moral panics) share a common characteristic:** They provide an **untestable (unassailable)** and **simple (but oversimplified)** explanation for a problem. The contagion provides an *ideology* that cuts uncertainty during anxious times.

**The mechanism:**
1. Something in the environment creates anxiety (perceived uncertainty + urgency)
2. People seek ways to reduce uncertainty
3. An untestable, oversimplified ideology emerges that "explains" the problem
4. People *perceive* uncertainty has decreased → they calm down
5. Short-term: the ideology solves the suffering of anxiety for believers
6. Long-term: it obscures and fails to address the true problem (which is: these problems aren't well understood)
7. The most intense cases do so much damage they create more problems than they solve

**The band-aid analogy:** One person reacts to a cut knee by calmly applying a band-aid. Another runs into the house screaming bloody murder, breaking furniture, distressing their spouse to the point where they leave the stove on rushing to the hospital, burning the house down, leaving the family homeless. The anxious response creates more damage than the original problem.

**Important:** These forces are *real*, serve a *function*, probably a *biological* function. They are not to be taken lightly or used as convenient rebellion. The question is not whether they exist, but whether we can recognize them and respond more thoughtfully.
