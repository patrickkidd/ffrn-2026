## PART 1: Patrick Solo (15 min)

### I. The Spirit of Discovery (5 min)

What inspired this work. Not a recap of the day — the personal root.

- There are people across very different fields who share a certain posture toward their work. Sagan said "somewhere, something incredible is waiting to be known." Feynman: "I would rather have questions that can't be answered than answers that can't be questioned." Bowen spent 40 years pursuing a science of human behavior — a goal "generally considered to be unattainable." At the end of his career he wrote about the human probing the secrets of the universe, sending himself to the moon on theory alone. Then turning inward: we have "hardly scratched the surface" of the space inside our own skulls. *"The development of the human brain will not permit him to stop."*
- These are people who found the act of discovery itself to be the reward. Not the specific concepts they landed on — the *posture*. The future is limitless. There is still so much to discover.
- That's the spirit behind everything I showed you today. Not "I have the answers." Rather: I built some tools for asking better questions, and the process of asking them has been one of the most rewarding things I've done.

### II. What Becomes Possible (5 min)

Not a pitch. A picture of what opens up when more people get curious enough to try.

**The personal payoff of coding cases:**
- The act of coding a case — "what shifted, when, in relation to what?" — changes how you think. Everyone in the working group has reported this.
- It's a practice, not a one-time insight. The more you do it, the clearer your mental model of the family becomes — not a vague impression, a structured picture.
- A clinician with a clear mental model makes better decisions — what to ask, what to leave alone, when something has shifted. Without one, you're navigating by feel, and feel is unreliable when anxiety is high.
- You don't need software. Pencil and a timeline. The software just makes it faster.
- Once you start tracking shifts instead of labeling states, you see process everywhere — your cases, your own family, the news.

→ **"That's not a technology. That's a way of using your mind."**

**The data starts to compound:**
- Right now every clinician is an island. Their observations die with their caseload.
- Shared variables → observations accumulate across practitioners. Knowledge compounds.
- Trainee in Brazil and a trainee in Alaska code the same case and get comparable results — not same opinions, same variables.

**The scale problem — tens of thousands of cases:**
- Era 2 — where AI starts finding patterns humans can't see, generating new hypotheses for humans to test — requires a critical mass of coded cases. Not dozens. Not hundreds. Tens of thousands.
- That's not one lab. That's a distributed network of clinicians coding real cases using the same variables.
- Every case you code is a data point the field has never had before. The more people coding, the faster we get to the point where the data starts teaching *us.*
- The human stays central — sets the standard, corrects the machine, decides what matters.

### III. The Invitation (5 min)

Concrete, brief, warm. Not a sales pitch. Resources for the curious.

**Start with your own thinking:**
- Before your next intake, draw the family. Put dates on the shifts. See what sequence emerges.
- The question isn't "what's wrong with this person" — it's "what shifted, when, in relation to what?"

**The tools exist:**
- The Pro app is available now — timeline + diagram, linked. familydiagram.com
- The Personal app is coming — your clients generate their own family assessment through conversation before they walk in.

**The seminar:**
- Monthly Zoom working group. Clinicians coding cases, sharpening each other's thinking on real data.
- Not lectures. Practice.

**Code cases for the study:**
- The IRR study needs trained coders. That's clinicians.
- You are the ground truth. You are the humans.

**Close:**

→ *"Bowen wrote at the end of his career that the journey was far more successful than he ever dreamed possible. I feel the same way about the last five years. None of what I showed you today existed five years ago. All of it works. And we've barely started."*

→ *"The field isn't hopeless — it's unmeasured. And unmeasured is fixable."*

→ *"Thank you for spending the day."*
