## III. What Becomes Possible (6 min)

Not a funding pitch. A picture of what opens up when more people get curious enough to try.

**The data starts to compound:**
- Right now, every clinician is an island. Their observations die with their caseload.
- With shared variables, observations accumulate across practitioners. Knowledge compounds.
- A trainee in Brazil and a trainee in Alaska evaluate the same case and get comparable results — not because they were taught the same opinions, but because they're looking at the same variables.

**The AI enters Era 2:**
- We talked this morning about Era 1: AI learns from humans. That's where we are now.
- Era 2 follows when enough ground truth accumulates: AI starts finding patterns humans couldn't see. New hypotheses for humans to test.
- The human remains central — setting the standard, correcting the machine, deciding what matters.

**The thinking spreads:**
- This is the part that excites me most.
- The act of coding a case — sitting down and asking "what shifted, when, and in relation to what?" — changes how you think. Every person in the working group has reported this.
- And it's not a one-time insight. It's a practice. The more you do it, the clearer you get about each case. You start to hold a coherent mental model of the family — not a vague impression, a structured picture.
- That matters clinically. A clinician with a clear mental model of the family makes better decisions — what to ask, what to leave alone, when something has shifted. A clinician without one is navigating by feel, and feel is unreliable when anxiety is high.
- You don't need software to do it. You need a pencil and a timeline. The software just makes it faster.
- Once you start thinking this way — tracking shifts instead of labeling states — you start seeing process everywhere. In your cases, in your own family, in the news.
- That's not a technology. That's a way of using your mind.

## IV. The Next Generation (6 min)

Deliver on Pres 3's promise: "More on this at day's end."

### You don't have to be an engineer.

This work needs people at every level, and most of the levels are clinical, not technical.

**Start with your own thinking:**
- Before your next intake, draw the family. Put dates on the shifts. See what sequence emerges.
- You don't need the app to do this. A whiteboard works. The app just remembers for you.
- The question isn't "what's wrong with this person." The question is "what shifted, when, and in relation to what?"

**Use the tools:**
- The Pro app is available now — timeline + diagram, linked.
- The Personal app is coming — your clients generate their own family assessment through conversation before they walk in.

**Join the seminar:**
- Monthly Zoom. Clinicians using the app, coding cases, sharpening each other's thinking.
- Not a lecture series. A working group. People learning to see process by practicing on real cases.

**Code cases for the IRR study:**
- The study needs trained coders. That's clinicians.
- You listen to a case, code it using SARF, your coding gets compared to others.
- This is the ground truth pipeline. Humans set the standard. You are the humans.

**Bring your observations:**
- You see patterns in your practice that no one else sees. Right now those observations exist only in your head.
- SARF gives you a way to record them so they can be tested — by you, by others, by machines.
- That's how science works. Not grand revelations. Patient observation, recorded clearly enough that someone else can check it.
